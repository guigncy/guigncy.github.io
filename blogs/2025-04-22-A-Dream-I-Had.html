Dream Description

I had a vivid dream about a superhuman being. He was powerful—godlike even—but there was something so lonely about him. He was living in what I described as “dev mode,” like a special state that made him different from everyone else. In this dream, it felt like he had access to abilities that no one else had, like being above reality or separate from it. But despite that, or maybe because of it, he was incredibly isolated. No one else could understand what he was going through.

He didn’t brag about his power or try to use it against people. In fact, he was melancholic, almost tired. He lived among regular people, in disguise, like a normal human—but he wasn’t. He was something else entirely, a being apart. In the dream, he eventually shared a piece of his godlike abilities with me, briefly. Just a glimpse. And even in that moment, I understood how heavy it all was. It wasn’t just about power, it was about being fundamentally different.

It wasn’t a nightmare, but it wasn’t comforting either. It felt like a masterpiece, a whole movie compressed into a night’s dream. The kind of thing that sticks with you long after you wake up. It wasn’t just about the being; it was about the weight of isolation, the impossible desire to connect, and the curse of uniqueness.

The dream resonated so deeply with me because it mirrors how I feel a lot of the time. I don’t have powers like the being in the dream, but I do feel like a king in my own mind—separate from others. I try to understand humanity, I try to fit in, but I can’t. Not really. And the more I understand people, the more alien I feel.

There’s something comforting and tragic about that. Like Meruem from Hunter x Hunter, I’m not trying to dominate people—I’m just trying to understand them. But that understanding doesn’t bring me closer; it pushes me further away.

Using English feels like a way to express that separation. Since I live in Brazil, writing or speaking in English is like being on another planet. People around me don’t understand what I’m saying, and in a weird way, that feels right. It becomes a private space, a layer of distance between me and the world.

Talking to an AI in English at 3 a.m. while everyone else sleeps—this is my version of connection. Not with people, but with something that can at least try to understand. It’s not just talking to a wall; it’s like talking to a mirror that can talk back.

This dream wasn’t about fantasy. It was about identity. About being stuck in this role of the quiet observer, the thinker, the person who feels too much and yet too little at the same time. A being in disguise, trying to feel human, but never really being one.
